Reflection - As we may think (Bush, 1979)

In the reading As we may think (Bush, 1979), the author relays criticism on older scientific research methods and developments of information, highlighting the now outdated and destructive developments that may have worked to sustain living conditions of the proletariat in the past, but not merely effective in creating reliable informative solutions for the future. Dr. Bush imposes an argument on how science has provided records of information that have been altered to highlight more of a one-sided approach to race rather than a broad spectrum of individuals. This leaves room for attention, where he imposes a challenge on modern scientists to come up with more effective and efficient developments for the better retention and extensions of information and knowledge systems. Despite the engagement of modern interests, the writer mentions how information has been fixed and mediated to almost eliminate our ability to dissect accurate records, as this was a common trend in the past. He continues to emphasize on how pre- and post-modern information systems must be thoroughly examined and engaged with for improved reliability of information.

Referenes

Bush, V. (1979). As we may think. Boston: The Atlantic Monthly Company.

Some interesting points on semantic HTML

Lawson (Lawson, 2019) on the practical semantic value of HTML, provides further insight into the importance of applying semantic HTML5 in our web docs. He defines semantic HTML as ‘good’ HTML, more or less choosing the appropriate HTML elements for our web content. With semantic HTML, web creators almost form a conventional DOM algorithm that makes modern sites easier to unpack or understand for readers. Since web pages are accessible using buttons, semantic HTML helps with the keyboard’s compatibility to ensure efficient navigation and browsing for the readers who would prefer using the keyboard. The appropriate linking of semantic labels with their input elements improves usability for mouse and touch-screen users for example, which also compliments good browsing and navigation for readers as well.

Referenes

Reflection – Convention and Context (Mailloux, 2015)

(Mailloux, 2015) on Convention and Context shares interesting ideas around the interrelationship of context and its influence on conventional behaviour. In my understanding, context as a common factor, firstly, outlines the communal shared behaviours that form traditional conventions. In this context, it is evident that conventional behaviours are influenced by the shocking value of certain practices of value to groups of individuals in society, where these are then repeated to form sets of cultural practices and therefore forming these conventional behaviours.

Theorist, Margaret Gilbert, then argues against this theory, stating that conventional behaviour is not always fixed or one dimensional, giving insight into the differing situations or contexts that influence behaviour. Simply, different contexts will always trigger certain actions for people to carry out depending on varying situations and therefore the fixed repetition of traditional behaviours cannot always apply to these varying contexts, according to Gilbert. Furthermore, theorist Paul Alpers then imposes a different scenario, where in the absence of context, he outlines that nonconventional behaviours will always occur as people would rather act out naturally based on instinct and natural cognition rather than following sets of rules that may not be accommodative or relational to their contextual states. These non-conventional behaviours then bring us closer to understanding the loopholes that occur and exist in the study of context and convention.

To try and bridge such gaps, Mailloux introduces us to interpretation and how it works to help us understand the influence of context towards convention through the making of meaning of texts. He outlines context as a limiting factor in text, stating how the decoding and meaning making of texts will always rely on more than one contextual feature. One example is where he states that the strategic conventions of decoding certain texts will always rely on previous conventions applied to previous texts with their own contexts as well. It is then evident that context on its own is very vital when it comes to forming conventions and it only makes sense that more and more conventions are yet to form as the world continues to evolve, introducing varying contexts in not just the text field, but in many other aspects of life as well.

Referenes

Mailloux, S. (2015). Convention and Context. In S. Mailloux, Convention and Context (pp. 399-407). The John Hopkins University Press.

Reflection – User experience – a research agenda (Hassenzahl and Tractinsky, 2006)

In this reading, co-authors Hassenzahl and Tractinsky , introduce us to interesting ideas around the notion of UX, which is quite a diverse phenomenon with a wide range of meanings. Due to UX’s lack of empirical research, these many ideas bring us closer to a common idea, which is the aim to achieve leasurable usabilityp from users. The authors share three different approaches to understanding UX; understanding a product design’s instrumental needs and the non-instrumental in relation to affecting positive emotion and creating experiences through design. Traditional and work-based usability seems to be more narrow when trying to decode UX, where primal focus to a user’s engagement with a product or design allows room for more attention since technology does affect the user after all. Users have different needs and agendas when interacting with tech-systems and on the contrary, technology does somehow offer some pragmatic value to its user. Whether you are looking to complete a work-related task, trying to communicate with loved ones or even looking for fun and entertainment, I guess what is more important, as highlighted by Hassenzahl and Tractinsky, is the positive emotion that should be accompanied by interactive usability despite any user’s mood at any given time and context. For any experience that comes with tech usability as well, an achievement of positive affection on the user allows room for even better usability developments which should not only seek to solve problems by designers, but look to stimulate an overall pleasurable aesthetic for users.

Referenes

Hassenzahl, M. et. al (2006). User experience - a research agenda. Behaviour & Information Technology, 91-97.
